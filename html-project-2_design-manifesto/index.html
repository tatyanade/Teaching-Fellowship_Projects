Paying attention to font choice is extremely important!

Generally we only want to use 2 fonts - the less we use the cleaner our site looks.

Generally, the best rule of thumb is to stick with one font for your titles and headers, like a serif font or a display font, and another font for the rest of your text, such as paragraphs or subtitles.

Heirarchy Hijinks

When writing content for a website, it can be easy to write every single thought you have and throw it onto a page. However, we want to pay special attention to how our content is placed on the screen.

Note the difference between the next three examples. All contain the same text but have varying degrees of legibility.

If I have a lot of text on the page, without any heirarchy or division of content, or without any labelling, or without any visual differentiation, such as size, color, weight, or font, or a lot of content contained in extremely long run-on sentances, the important parts of what I am trying to say might get lost in the ocean of text that I am presenting to the viewer, and they may miss a lot of what I want to show them, especially the important parts, because they are jumbled into an unclimbable wall of text that becomes imposing and unreadable and absolutely prevents any sort of meaning or wisdom to be discerned from it, regardless of how much I, the writer of all the content, desprately wishes that the reader of my webpage, in this case you, would go through all my words, memorize them by heart, and accept them willingly into their mental lexicon of design principles.

    Image Irritation

    Images can add a lot to your page! After all, an image is worth one thousand words. Here are some guidelines:

    It can look jarring to have lots of images of different weights and heights.

    This looks bad! It is better to keep all of the heights the same if we are storing images in a row (like google images), or to keep the widths the same if we are storing in columns (like pinterest).

    When using images, we want to make sure our image quality is proportional to the size we display it as. Observe the difference between small images made bigger, and bigger images made smaller.
